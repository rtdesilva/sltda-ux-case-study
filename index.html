Background

Understanding the Context

SLTDA is Sri Lanka's apex government body for tourism regulation, licensing, and policy. Its website serves a dual audience: international tourists seeking credibility-building information and industry stakeholders seeking regulatory guidance — yet its UX fails both.

The International Traveller

Primary Audience

A European or Asian tourist planning a first visit to Sri Lanka. Needs visa info, certified accommodation, and things-to-do guidance.

Goals
  • Find SLTDA-approved hotels
  • Understand visa requirements
  • Build trip confidence & safety trust
Frustrations with SLTDA
  • Broken image links across pages
  • No clear itinerary or inspiration
  • Downloads heavy PDFs instead of web pages

The Tourism Operator

Secondary Audience

A hotel or tour operator who needs to register with SLTDA, pay levies, or access regulatory updates and compliance notices.

Goals
  • Register business with SLTDA
  • Download compliance forms
  • Track tourism development levies
Frustrations with SLTDA
  • Registration process unclear, multi-step
  • Forms buried in download section
  • No status-tracking for applications

The Policy Researcher

Tertiary Audience

A journalist, academic, or government analyst seeking Sri Lanka's tourism statistics, policy papers, and industry data.

Goals
  • Access annual arrivals data
  • Download policy documents
  • Find sustainability reports
Frustrations with SLTDA
  • Stats section is hard to find
  • No interactive data visualisation
  • Publications scattered, undated
UX Audit

What's Working & What's Broken

A first-principles review of the current SLTDA website across six critical UX dimensions, revealing structural and aesthetic issues that damage credibility and usability.

Critical Issue

Poor Mobile Responsiveness

The site's layout breaks on mobile devices. Navigation is cramped, images are disproportionate, and the video background hero creates lag on low-bandwidth connections common in target tourist markets (India, China, Russia).

Critical Issue

Broken & Missing Assets

Multiple slider images return broken links (empty img tags without valid src attributes). This creates a visually jarring experience and signals poor maintenance to first-time visitors, eroding institutional credibility.

Critical Issue

Inadequate Search Functionality

The search icon is decorative with no functional dropdown or results page. Users cannot search for specific hotels, regulations, or documents. There's no typeahead, autocomplete, or filtering — a critical failure for a regulatory site.

Warning

Confusing Information Architecture

The navigation mixes government-facing content (Downloads, Register, Statistics) with visitor-facing content without clear audience segmentation. New visitors don't know if this is a tourist site or a business regulation portal.

Warning

Outdated Visual Identity

The design language is circa 2010 — flat sliders, generic typography, no design system coherence. Compared to competitors like visitsingapore.com, the aesthetic underrepresents the extraordinary natural beauty of Sri Lanka.

Warning

Accessibility Gaps

Low colour contrast ratios in navigation links, no visible skip-to-content link, missing alt text on key images, and video backgrounds without pause controls fail WCAG 2.1 AA standards.

Warning

PDF-First Content Strategy

Critical information (visa details, industry notices, policy papers) is locked in PDF downloads rather than responsive web pages. PDFs create friction, cannot be indexed well by search engines, and are inaccessible on mobile.

Positive

Multilingual Support

The site offers Sinhala and Tamil language support alongside English — an inclusive approach for local industry stakeholders. This is an important foundation that should be preserved and extended in any redesign.

Positive

Emergency Contact Visibility

The Tourism Hotline (1912) and Emergency Ambulance (1990) are clearly displayed in the footer — demonstrating user safety awareness. These should be elevated to the header for mobile users in a redesign.

Key Insight: Singapore Tourism Board's website serves as the gold standard — it maintains a clear separation between its corporate/B2B portal (stb.gov.sg) and its consumer travel inspiration site (visitsingapore.com). SLTDA conflates both audiences in a single site with no clear wayfinding, which dilutes the experience for both. Visit Maldives demonstrates that even a smaller island nation can deliver a luxury, image-driven site that competes globally. Sri Lanka's extraordinary visual assets — ancient temples, lush rainforests, tea country, pristine beaches — are almost entirely absent from the SLTDA homepage.

Nielsen's 10 Heuristics

Heuristic Evaluation

Scoring the SLTDA site against Nielsen's ten usability heuristics — the industry standard framework for expert UX review.

01

Visibility of System Status

No loading indicators, no progress feedback for multi-step registration flows, no visual confirmation when downloading files. The video banner plays silently with no status cue.

2/5 — Poor
02

Match Between System and Real World

Bureaucratic language dominates ("Tourism Development Levy," "TDL Notices") without plain-language explanations. The site speaks in the vocabulary of a government ministry, not a tourism destination.

2/5 — Poor
03

User Control & Freedom

Pop-up modals (for notices and fuel assistance programs) have a close button but the cookie banner persists aggressively. Deep navigation has no clear back-path — users must rely on the browser back button.

3/5 — Average
04

Consistency & Standards

Button styles vary inconsistently across sections. Some sections use card-based layouts while others use raw text links. Typography scales are not standardised — headings range widely in size without a clear hierarchy.

2/5 — Poor
05

Error Prevention

The external registration portal (register with SLTDA) has no clearly visible error states or input validation hints visible from the landing page. PDFs are offered without file size indicators.

3/5 — Average
06

Recognition over Recall

No breadcrumb navigation, no persistent sidebar, and no contextual cross-links mean users must memorise their location within the site. The download section has no categorised listing — users scroll through an undifferentiated list.

2/5 — Poor
07

Flexibility & Efficiency of Use

No keyboard shortcuts, no saved preferences, no personalisation layer. Expert users (industry operators) cannot filter news/notices by category. There is no API or data portal for researchers.

1/5 — Critical Fail
08

Aesthetic & Minimalist Design

The homepage is dense with competing information blocks: popups, sliders, link lists, partner logos, and emergency contacts all competing for attention. The signal-to-noise ratio is critically low.

2/5 — Poor
09

Help Users Recognise & Recover from Errors

Broken image links produce blank spaces with no fallback, no alt text, and no error message. Users cannot determine whether missing content is intentional or a system error.

1/5 — Critical Fail
10

Help & Documentation

The ISLA chatbot icon is present but its capabilities are unclear. There is no FAQ section visible from the homepage, no guided onboarding for first-time visitors, and no contextual help tooltips.

2/5 — Poor

Journey Failure Point: The core value proposition of SLTDA — being the trusted certification authority — is completely undeliverable from the website's current UX. The SLTDA-approved accommodation database exists but is hidden on a sister site (srilanka.travel) with zero visible wayfinding from the main homepage. This structural split between the two portals is the single highest-impact UX problem to solve.

Design Recommendations

Priority Improvements

A prioritised set of design and strategy recommendations — ordered by user impact and implementation feasibility.

Priority 1 — Immediate

Audience Bifurcation: Two Clear Entry Points

Redesign the homepage to present two primary CTAs: "I'm a Visitor to Sri Lanka" and "I'm a Tourism Industry Professional." This mirrors Singapore STB's approach and immediately reduces cognitive load for all users. Each path leads to a tailored sub-experience.

Priority 1 — Immediate

Fix Broken Assets & Performance

Audit and repair all broken image links, reduce homepage video size with a static fallback for mobile, and implement lazy loading. Target a sub-3s page load on 4G. This is table-stakes credibility for a government authority site.

Priority 1 — Immediate

Functional Global Search with Filters

Implement a full-text search with category filters (Hotels, Regulations, News, Statistics). Include autocomplete for accommodation names and document types. This alone would resolve the most common user journeys that currently dead-end.

Priority 3 — Medium Term

Interactive Statistics Dashboard

Strategic Vision

A Phased Roadmap

Phase 01 0–3 months

Stabilise & Repair

Fix broken assets, implement search, reduce page load to under 3s. Set up WCAG 2.1 AA audit and address critical failures.

Phase 02 3–9 months

Redesign & Restructure

Visual overhaul, audience bifurcation, mobile-first rebuild, conversion of PDFs to web content. New design system implementation.

Phase 03 9–18 months

Innovate & Elevate

Interactive statistics dashboard, ISLA 2.0 AI concierge, B2B industry portal, social integration, and personalisation layer.

Phase 04 18–36 months

Lead & Benchmark

Ongoing user research programme, A/B testing infrastructure, annual UX audits benchmarked against STB Singapore.

Sri Lanka possesses an extraordinary product — ancient civilisations, pristine coastlines, endemic wildlife, and world-class cuisine. The gap between this reality and its digital representation is a missed opportunity of significant economic consequence.

Singapore's STB generates measurable ROI from digital investment — interactive platforms, AI-driven analytics, and separated B2B tools directly support their quality tourism strategy. SLTDA's website, in contrast, actively works against Sri Lanka's tourism ambitions by presenting an outdated, broken, and confusing digital front door to millions of potential visitors and investors annually.

The investment required to bring SLTDA's digital presence to regional par is modest relative to the potential increase in high-value visitor confidence, industry compliance rates, and Sri Lanka's competitive positioning as a post-2022 economic recovery story in South Asian tourism.
